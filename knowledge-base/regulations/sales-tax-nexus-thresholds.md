# Sales Tax Economic Nexus — State-by-State Thresholds (2026)

Reference file for multistate sales-tax economic-nexus analysis. Pairs with the (forthcoming) **Sales Tax Nexus Analyzer** skill, the **Tax Memo Writer**, and any advisory engagement with a remote-seller fact pattern.

**Authority.** *South Dakota v. Wayfair, Inc.*, 585 U.S. 162 (2018), overruled the physical-presence rule of *Quill Corp. v. North Dakota*, 504 U.S. 298 (1992), and *National Bellas Hess, Inc. v. Dep't of Revenue of Illinois*, 386 U.S. 753 (1967). States may now require out-of-state sellers to collect and remit sales tax based on economic activity alone.

**Scope.** This page summarizes statutory economic-nexus thresholds adopted by each state and the District of Columbia, the measurement period each state uses, and whether marketplace-facilitated sales count toward the threshold. **Always confirm against the state department-of-revenue notice or statute before registering or filing** — thresholds are amended regularly and post-Wayfair the trend has been toward eliminating transaction-count tests.

---

## Core Concepts

- **Sales threshold** — Dollar amount of gross, retail, or taxable sales (varies by state) into the state in the measurement period that triggers a registration / collection obligation.
- **Transaction threshold** — Number of separate transactions into the state. Historically common; many states have repealed these since 2019.
- **Threshold logic** — Most states trigger nexus if **either** the dollar or transaction test is met (OR logic). New York and a few others require **both** to be met (AND logic). Confirm before registering.
- **Measurement period** — The lookback window the state uses. Common variants: previous calendar year; current or previous calendar year; preceding 12-month rolling period; previous four sales-tax quarters.
- **Marketplace-facilitated sales** — Sales routed through Amazon, Etsy, Walmart Marketplace, etc. Most states exclude these from the remote-seller's threshold because the marketplace facilitator is responsible for collection. A handful still count them toward the threshold even though the seller does not collect — confirm by state.
- **Physical nexus still applies** — Inventory in a 3PL warehouse, employees, contractors, or trade-show attendance can independently create nexus regardless of the economic thresholds below.

## State-by-State Economic Nexus Thresholds

Format: **State — Sales threshold / Transactions threshold / Measurement period / Notes.**

### States With No Statewide Sales Tax
These states impose no general sales tax at the state level. Local or gross-receipts taxes may still apply (notably Alaska local sales taxes and the New Mexico Gross Receipts Tax, which is treated as a sales tax for nexus purposes).

- **Alaska** — No state sales tax. Local jurisdictions participate in the **Alaska Remote Seller Sales Tax Commission (ARSSTC)** with a **$100,000** economic-nexus threshold (previous calendar year, statewide). Local transaction-count test was **eliminated** effective 2025.
- **Delaware** — No state sales tax. Gross-receipts tax is imposed on in-state sellers only.
- **Montana** — No state sales tax.
- **New Hampshire** — No state sales tax.
- **Oregon** — No state sales tax. Corporate Activity Tax (CAT) applies separately.

### States Using the $100,000 / 200-Transaction "Wayfair Baseline" (Dollar-OR-Transaction Trigger)

Unless noted, the measurement period is "previous or current calendar year" and the trigger fires when **either** test is met.

- **Arkansas** — $100,000 / 200 transactions / previous or current calendar year.
- **District of Columbia** — $100,000 / 200 transactions / previous or current calendar year.
- **Georgia** — $100,000 / 200 transactions / previous or current calendar year.
- **Hawaii** — $100,000 / 200 transactions / previous or current calendar year (General Excise Tax).
- **Idaho** — $100,000 / no transaction test / previous or current calendar year.
- **Illinois** — $100,000 / 200 transactions / preceding 12 months, measured quarterly.
- **Indiana** — $100,000 / no transaction test (repealed effective 2024) / previous or current calendar year.
- **Kentucky** — $100,000 / 200 transactions / previous or current calendar year.
- **Louisiana** — $100,000 / no transaction test (repealed effective August 1, 2023) / previous or current calendar year.
- **Maine** — $100,000 / no transaction test (repealed effective 2022) / previous or current calendar year.
- **Maryland** — $100,000 / 200 transactions / previous or current calendar year.
- **Massachusetts** — $100,000 / no transaction test / previous or current calendar year.
- **Michigan** — $100,000 / 200 transactions / previous calendar year.
- **Minnesota** — $100,000 / 200 transactions / preceding 12 months.
- **Nebraska** — $100,000 / 200 transactions / previous or current calendar year.
- **Nevada** — $100,000 / 200 transactions / previous or current calendar year.
- **New Jersey** — $100,000 / 200 transactions / previous or current calendar year.
- **North Carolina** — $100,000 / no transaction test (repealed effective July 1, 2024) / previous or current calendar year.
- **North Dakota** — $100,000 / no transaction test / previous or current calendar year.
- **Ohio** — $100,000 / 200 transactions / previous or current calendar year.
- **Pennsylvania** — $100,000 / no transaction test / previous 12 months.
- **Rhode Island** — $100,000 / 200 transactions / previous calendar year.
- **South Carolina** — $100,000 / no transaction test / previous or current calendar year.
- **South Dakota** — $100,000 / no transaction test (repealed effective July 1, 2023) / previous or current calendar year. Note: this is the state whose statute was tested in *Wayfair*.
- **Utah** — $100,000 / 200 transactions / previous or current calendar year.
- **Vermont** — $100,000 / 200 transactions / preceding 12 months.
- **Virginia** — $100,000 / 200 transactions / previous or current calendar year.
- **Washington** — $100,000 / no transaction test / current or previous calendar year (B&O and sales tax).
- **West Virginia** — $100,000 / 200 transactions / previous or current calendar year.
- **Wisconsin** — $100,000 / no transaction test / previous or current calendar year.
- **Wyoming** — $100,000 / no transaction test (repealed effective July 1, 2024) / previous or current calendar year.

### States With Higher Sales-Only Thresholds

- **California** — **$500,000** / no transaction test / previous or current calendar year. Combined statewide — use *total* sales into California, including marketplace-facilitated.
- **New York** — **$500,000 AND 100 transactions** (both must be met) / immediately preceding four sales-tax quarters.
- **Texas** — **$500,000** / no transaction test / previous 12 calendar months. Reg. allows safe-harbor exit once threshold not met for 12 consecutive months.

### States With Mid-Band Thresholds

- **Alabama** — **$250,000** / no transaction test / previous calendar year. Simplified Sellers Use Tax (SSUT) program at 8% flat rate available.
- **Mississippi** — **$250,000** / no transaction test / any 12-month period.
- **Connecticut** — **$100,000 AND 200 transactions** (both must be met) / preceding 12-month period ending September 30.
- **Tennessee** — **$100,000** / no transaction test (transaction test repealed effective October 1, 2020) / previous 12 months.
- **Florida** — **$100,000** / no transaction test / previous calendar year.
- **Kansas** — **$100,000** / no transaction test (2021 amendment) / current or previous calendar year.
- **Missouri** — **$100,000** / no transaction test / previous 12 months (rolling). Effective January 1, 2023 under S.B. 153.
- **New Mexico** — **$100,000** / no transaction test / previous calendar year (Gross Receipts Tax).
- **Oklahoma** — **$100,000** / no transaction test / previous 12 months.
- **Iowa** — **$100,000** / no transaction test (transaction test repealed effective 2019) / previous or current calendar year.
- **Arizona** — **$100,000** / no transaction test (reduced from $150K effective January 1, 2021) / previous or current calendar year.
- **Colorado** — **$100,000** / no transaction test / previous or current calendar year. **Home-rule complication**: Colorado has more than 70 home-rule cities that administer their own sales taxes; registration with the Sales & Use Tax System (SUTS) covers most but not all.

### Notable Multi-Tier Frameworks

- **Puerto Rico** — $100,000 / 200 transactions / previous or current calendar year (IVU, territorial tax).

## "AND" vs. "OR" States At-a-Glance

Most states apply OR logic (either test triggers nexus). The following apply AND logic (both must be met):

- **Connecticut** — $100,000 AND 200 transactions.
- **New York** — $500,000 AND 100 transactions.

Some states have split behavior — the above is correct for the general economic-nexus rule; always confirm against the DOR notice.

## Marketplace-Facilitator Treatment

Every state with a sales tax now has a marketplace-facilitator law. Default rule: the **marketplace facilitator** (Amazon, eBay, Etsy, Walmart Marketplace, etc.) collects and remits on sales it facilitates.

- **Excluded from remote-seller threshold** in most states: California, New Jersey, Illinois, Massachusetts, Michigan, Ohio, Pennsylvania, Virginia, and a majority of others.
- **Included in remote-seller threshold** in a smaller set of states even though facilitator collects: confirm with the DOR before assuming. Historically New York, Connecticut, and several others included them; rules have shifted.
- **Practical implication** — A seller that does 90% of its volume through Amazon can still trigger nexus registration obligations in states that count marketplace sales toward the threshold, even if the seller itself never collects. The seller may still need to register to file information returns or zero-dollar returns.

## Trailing Obligations Beyond Collection

Triggering economic nexus creates several obligations, not all of which terminate when the threshold is no longer met:

1. **Register** with the state's DOR — timing varies (immediately, next quarter, first day of following month).
2. **Collect and remit** tax at the correct combined state + local rate (destination-based sourcing in most states; origin-based in a minority such as Texas and Pennsylvania for in-state sellers).
3. **File returns** at the cadence assigned (monthly / quarterly / annual based on tax due).
4. **Manage exemption certificates** (resale, nonprofit, government, manufacturing) — multi-state sellers often use the SSTGB Certificate of Exemption.
5. **De-register** — Most states require continued filing for a period after sales drop below the threshold (typically one to two calendar years or until the measurement window rolls off).
6. **Back-tax exposure** — If the taxpayer crossed the threshold in a prior year and did not register, voluntary disclosure agreement (VDA) programs can limit lookback (typically to three or four years) and abate penalties. Most states do not abate collected-but-unremitted tax.

## Streamlined Sales Tax (SST) Program

Twenty-four SST member states accept a single registration through the Streamlined Sales Tax Registration System (SSTRS). Member states cover a majority of U.S. population and include most of the baseline-threshold states above (Arkansas, Georgia, Indiana, Iowa, Kansas, Kentucky, Michigan, Minnesota, Nebraska, Nevada, New Jersey, North Carolina, North Dakota, Ohio, Oklahoma, Rhode Island, South Dakota, Tennessee, Utah, Vermont, Washington, West Virginia, Wisconsin, Wyoming).

## Working Template — Nexus Review Output

When producing a nexus memo or registration plan, the output should follow this structure:

1. **Executive summary** — States where the taxpayer currently has nexus (physical and economic), states it is approaching a threshold, and states where it has no obligation.
2. **Nexus schedule** — Per state, columns: sales into state / transaction count / threshold / trigger date / registration due / SSUT-eligible / marketplace-excluded / filing cadence / first-return period.
3. **Physical-nexus exposure** — Inventory (Amazon FBA state map is the common source), employees, contractors, property.
4. **Back-tax exposure and VDA candidates** — States with likely prior-year exposure and whether a VDA is advantageous.
5. **Registration plan** — Priority order, resource estimate (hours to register + testing), and tool recommendations (Avalara, TaxJar, Stripe Tax, etc.).
6. **Ongoing compliance calendar** — Monthly / quarterly / annual filing dates, zero-return flags, and de-registration triggers.
